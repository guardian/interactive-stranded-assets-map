In a business-as-usual scenario, the researchers estimate the world would have $25trn of fossil fuel assets by 2036. The US, Russia and Opec countries would have the largest reserves of coal, oil and gas.

But their analysis shows that the true value of these assets is far lower because renewables are on course to replace them due to technological innovation, investments flows and government policies.

With net zero policies coming into effect, and demand for oil and gas declining, Opec countries may choose to increase production and sell fossil fuel assets at a high price while they still can.

This would devalue fossil fuel fields and infrastructure elsewhere. For example, 62% of US assets would be stranded compared to a business-as-usual scenario. Similar crashes are likely in other oil exporting nations.

In another scenario, Opec accepts quotas to prevent the fire sale of fossil fuels. This would still lead to about $11tn of stranded assets, although these losses would be distributed more evenly among producer countries.

This means the true value of fossil fuel assets would be around half that estimated today. This would lead to more than $1trn hits for each of the USA, Russia and China.

Despite the loss of fossil fuel assets in this net-zero scenario, the world economy would still grow by $44tn by 2036 - or 3.9%. Russia and China would see the value of their fossil fuel assets fall, but their economies would still increase overall.

The outlook for developing nations is even rosier, with countries including Argentina, Kazakhstan and India projected to record double-digit economic growth. But the US, Canada and Norway would be among the net losers because so much of their oil and gas assets are expensive to extract.
